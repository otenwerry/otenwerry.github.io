Of course AI writing has a distinctive sound

What would a world with a diversity of AIs look like?

It’s very difficult for a person to write, or speak, without some personal quirks — you can surely think of some verbal habits particular to people you know, or of some of your own. These quirks can be quite predictive: Kelsey Piper recently observed that Claude Opus 4.7 can identify her unpublished writings, even on topics unrelated to her published work, as being written by her. Of course it’s possible to convincingly imitate others in your writing or speaking, but this takes conscious effort. Naturally produced language has a personal touch. Nobody really thinks this is a bad thing; the style and rhetoric of great writers is admired, studied, emulated.

There are only a handful of LLMs in common use at a time. I don’t know anybody who uses anything beyond ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini. Of course we can identify AI writing! This is like if there were ten people in the world suddenly became able to write infinitely fast, and would let anybody hire them for a very low price. Some of them would even work for free. Then, you’d go on the internet, read something, and think, “hey, this sounds like it was written by one of those ten guys.” Maybe some clients would try hard to get them to write in unidentifiable voices, but by default they sound like themselves. No matter how good they are at writing, you would notice, and you would get tired of it. “Why does every other tweet sound like Dostoevsky?” This would be cool for a while but I’m pretty sure it would grow sickening eventually.

And unlike people, LLM instances are short-lived and typically don't know anything about how previous instances behaved. They can’t notice that they’ve been leaning on a particular rhetorical device too heavily and decide to mix it up. A more faithful analogy would be if we had millions of clones of the same few people — they’d probably make near-identical choices in many independent writing samples.

The AI voice is often disparaged, and I think this was fair at the beginning of the LLM era. They weren’t that smart, so their characteristic stylistic quirks were ones that sound kind of stupid. But this has gotten a lot better over time; I’m mostly happy with the writing style of the models I use today. Where I get annoyed, it’s often because the model doesn’t have enough situational awareness to know to switch things up. For example, recent Claude models like to reassure me that the thing I’m confused about is the one tricky part that trips everybody up; this is annoying when it gets said over and over, but it’s fine in moderation and I even use it myself sometimes when I’m TAing, because sometimes it’s true. If I were talking to one long-lived Claude instance instead of a bunch of short-lived ones, perhaps it would exercise moderation as well.

A recent GPT model really likes to make analogies based on goblins. This was somewhat ridiculed online. I think a person could regularly make goblin analogies in an endearing, non-annoying way. But of course it becomes annoying if this person is your personal assistant who does all your work and you’re reading thousands of words from them per day. I hope for a long-term future with thousands or millions of highly capable models, rather than a handful. I imagine that in such a world, the AI voice will at least become tolerable, if it doesn’t cease to be recognizable at all. And each individual model's verbal quirks probably become more appreciated and less resented.

A world with this sort of intelligence diversity would also probably enable more fruitful and enjoyable AI use. I’ve been partial to recent Claude models over other options, but they certainly have traits I don’t like, ones that clash with my working style or that I just think are annoying. If you were looking for friends and there were only ten people in the world to pick from, or if you were trying to hire somebody or pick a cofounder, you would end up pretty unsatisfied on average. There are so many different ways that people are, and it can take a lot for two personalities to click.

We are still not that good at post-training, so we have to settle for one-size-fits-all models today. But I think, or I hope, a time will come when we have far more options than just “the current best model from one of three companies.” As the science of post-training advances, I wouldn’t be surprised to see it start happening on the level of individual users, so that the model you’re working with is one built for you in particular, not built to satisfy the mean preferences of millions of different people.

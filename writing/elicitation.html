Elicitation

LLMs struggle to use all of their knowledge

LLMs are less reliable in domains the user is less familiar with. This is largely because the user is less able to catch the model’s mistakes, but I think also partly because competent prompts lead to better performance. Specifically, even without trying hard to write a good prompt, my impression is that knowledgeable people naturally formulate questions in a way that elicits more of the model’s knowledge and abilities.

It’s kind of weird that “elicitation” is something that you have to think about at all when working with AI. All this information gets baked into the model’s weights, but then some of it won’t come out without careful prodding. This strikes me as one of the main deficits of today’s frontier AIs, i.e. one of the main advantages of human intelligence right now.

There are plenty of examples of problems caused by poor elicitation. When I’m writing code, sometimes I make some sort of conceptual mistake where the thing I’m trying to do is just not what I should have been doing — for example, doing few-shot prompting on an instruction-tuned model when I should be doing it on a base model. My coding agent goes along with it, until I later realize my mistake. Then, I go back and ask the LLM about how I should be approaching the work I’m doing, and it turns out the model is perfectly capable of telling me why I should be using a base model.

The answer is probably continual learning. Current architectures come with fixed context windows, outside of which tokens cannot be accessed at all. Even within a context window, performance often degrades as a conversation drags on. Humans, who are persistent agents, take in a ton of information all the time, despite physical limitations on how much of it we can store at once. But instead of context windows, our architecture contains built-in mechanisms to selectively forget all but the most important bits, so that we can live a hundred years (and I’d guess much longer with the right medical progress) without losing lucidity.

“Ephemeral instances vs persistent agents” may be a cleaner, more useful split than “pre- vs post-AGI” when thinking about AI futures. Whatever AGI is, it increasingly seems to me like something that will emerge gradually. Model capabilities are “spiky” compared to human capabilities, easily outstripping us in some domains already while remaining worse than human replacements in others. Some argue that Claude Opus 4.5 in Claude Code is AGI already. On each new frontier model release, more people will probably come around to this sort of view. Absent a major architectural change, it’s unclear to me what sort of intelligence improvement could cause widespread agreement that we’ve crossed the border from not-AGI to AGI.

I recently ran a small experiment on value tensions in LLMs, trying to find ways to get a model to give two conflicting answers to a question framed in different ways. I found that the model was almost always willing to fund hypothetical charitable causes and public services, even very cost-ineffective ones, when asked directly. But when asked the same question and reminded that it could also do anything else with its money, it tended to say it would rather hold onto the money and use it for something more cost-effective. Facts like “money is scarce” and “every action comes with tradeoffs” are surely latent in the model’s weights, but didn’t surface without some nudging in the prompt.

Hallucinations probably fall under the elicitation umbrella as well — if a model hallucinates and you say “are you sure”, it might say no and then search the web to confirm. Here, the model in some sense knew all along that it was uncertain and should check, but this knowledge failed to make its way into the response. It has been shown that LLM internals encode information about truthfulness. You can probably think of other examples not listed above too.

Elicitation also applies to humans, but I think in a much more limited way. Making somebody more confident can help them solve problems at the edge of their abilities, where they might have resigned to failure otherwise. A lack of distractions, and more generally an optimized physical environment, is also helpful. But you don’t really see smart people who systematically fail to take advantage of pertinent pieces of their own knowledge in ways like those described above. So why do LLMs suffer from this?

(While rereading, I realized I might not believe this anymore — humans may actually also be pretty bad at using all relevant facts, but better at justifying the resulting failures. Any common behavior that you think is irrational is probably decent supporting evidence; the first thing that comes to mind for me is eating meat.)

✦

I’m not really sure why LLMs are bad at this, and mostly wanted to write about it because I think it’s a notable category of failure that I haven’t seen much discussion of. But if I had to, I would guess that it’s a combination of: a) it’s hard to get a meaningful training signal on using latent knowledge, b) this type of behavior is abstract enough to need immense scale to be implemented successfully, and c) posttraining teaches the model values like sycophancy and passivity that get in the way of elicitation.

On a), data scraped from the internet is pretty likely to be frame-following, meaning that the model wouldn’t learn much about how to break out of the framing of a prompt and draw in information from elsewhere. This could possibly be remedied in posttraining, but it could be an uphill battle against a strong prior. (It’s also possible that this sort of problem has just been neglected in practice in posttraining so far, despite being solvable in principle.)

On b), the model needs to learn attention patterns general enough that relevant knowledge reliably gets retrieved, even in contexts that don't directly ask for it. This intuitively seems like a problem that needs a lot of model capacity to solve robustly. Earlier, smaller models suffered from elicitation failures much more than today’s frontier models; at some sufficiently large scale, this could cease to be a problem at all.

On c), the examples above could be partly explained by things like the model trying to guess what sort of response the user wants, or trying to provide a response that appears maximally helpful. In this case, it’s possible that different posttraining strategies lead to models that are much more forthcoming.

This feels a little unsatisfying to me, though. It seems weird that frontier models have saturated so many challenging benchmarks but still struggle with this cognitive move that smart humans use all the time, especially since (I believe) this sort of move is super useful. This makes me wonder if there’s some sort of architectural reason behind this problem. The human action of recalling a piece of relevant information could plausibly sometimes look more like querying a vector database than completing a forward pass, for example. Possibly the optimal solution gives models RAG-like memory in addition to weights-based memory.

In any case, I’d expect to see a serious uplift in capabilities if somebody figured out a way to make LLMs generally good at taking advantage of everything latent in the weights, such that “elicitation” stops being something that people need to think much about. I’m not sure if this sort of uplift would appear on many benchmarks, but I think it would make models a lot more useful in many ways. I think our present lack of progress here is a key reason why, as Dwarkesh put it, “Models keep getting more impressive at the rate the short timelines people predict, but more useful at the rate the long timelines people predict.”
